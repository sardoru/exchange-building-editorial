## Introduction: Why a Storage Slip Runs the Commodity World

A warehouse receipt is, on its face, the humblest of documents: a custodian's acknowledgment that it holds someone else's goods. But when the law makes that acknowledgment a *document of title* — and especially when it makes it *negotiable* — the slip of paper becomes something far more powerful. The goods can be sold, financed, pledged, and re-pledged without ever leaving the shed. A bank can lend against a bale of cotton it has never seen, in a warehouse it has never visited, because it physically holds the paper that the law says *is* the cotton for commercial purposes. The receipt is the bridge between the physical commodity world and the financial world, and the entire architecture of commodity finance — from a Mississippi farmer's harvest loan to a multinational's trade line — rests on it.

That power has always come with a single, recurring question: can the claim be trusted as much as the goods themselves? Every era of warehouse receipt history is a new answer to that question, built with the technology of its day — wax seals, engraved certificates, punch cards, central databases, and now cryptographic tokens. Each transition was forced by a breaking point in the old system, and each new system inherited the old questions in new clothing.

The American cotton industry is the best laboratory for this history, because it ran the experiment first and most completely: it took a pure bearer-paper system, hit a spectacular operational wall in 1989, designed and litigated and argued the world's first full-scale negotiable electronic warehouse receipt (EWR) system into existence between 1984 and 1995, and converted essentially the entire crop to electronic title. The questions its merchants, bankers, warehousemen, and regulators fought over in a Memphis conference room in August 1994 are — almost line for line — the questions institutions are asking about tokenized real-world assets in 2026.

## Part I — The Paper Era (Antiquity to 1984)

### Granaries, Goldsmiths, and the Invention of the Claim

The warehouse receipt is older than coined money. In the granary economies of Egypt and Mesopotamia, deposits of grain at state and temple storehouses generated written acknowledgments that could settle debts and taxes; the claim on stored grain circulated in place of the grain itself. The same logic reappeared in seventeenth-century London, where goldsmiths who took deposits of coin and plate issued receipts that began to pass from hand to hand. Those goldsmiths' notes are the ancestors of both the banknote and the bearer warehouse receipt, and they established the core invention that every later system would refine: a trusted custodian's record standing in, legally and commercially, for absent goods.

### 1848–1877: Chicago Industrializes the Receipt

The modern American warehouse receipt was forged in the grain trade. The Chicago Board of Trade, founded in 1848, sat atop a network of steam-powered grain elevators that did something revolutionary: they commingled grain by standardized grade, so that a receipt no longer pointed to *your* sacks of wheat but to a quantity of fungible No. 2 Spring at a licensed elevator. That abstraction made grain tradable at distance, made the receipt a financial instrument, and made the elevator receipt the deliverable behind the first standardized futures contracts in 1865. It also created the first warehouse receipt scandals — receipts issued against empty bins, elevators trading against their own depositors, monopoly storage rates — and the first regulatory response. In *Munn v. Illinois* (1877), the U.S. Supreme Court upheld state regulation of grain warehouses as businesses affected with a public interest, establishing that the custodians of the nation's crops could not be left entirely to private virtue.

### 1906: The Uniform Warehouse Receipts Act

By the turn of the century, every state had its own warehouse receipt law, and the legal effect of a receipt — particularly whether a good-faith purchaser took free of prior claims — varied across the borders the grain itself crossed daily. The Uniform Warehouse Receipts Act of 1906, drafted by the National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws, gave the country a common framework: a clear distinction between negotiable receipts (deliverable to bearer or to the order of a named person, transferable by delivery or endorsement, conferring title on a holder by due negotiation) and non-negotiable receipts (mere evidence of a storage contract). Adopted eventually in every state, the UWRA made the receipt a reliable national instrument for the first time.

### 1914–1916: The Cotton Crisis and the United States Warehouse Act

The federal government entered the field because of cotton. When the First World War closed European markets in the summer of 1914, cotton prices collapsed and Southern farmers found they could not even borrow against their stored crop: lenders did not trust the patchwork of state-licensed and unlicensed warehouses, their insurance, their bonding, or their receipts. Congress's answer, signed on August 11, 1916, was the United States Warehouse Act — a voluntary federal licensing regime under which a warehouse operator could submit to USDA examination, bonding, insurance requirements, and licensed weighing and grading, in exchange for the right to issue federally backed receipts. The explicit goal, repeated in USDA literature for the next century, was to make the warehouse receipt as dependable as money, so that it could move through the banking system as readily as a check. The USWA receipt — and its state-licensed cousins built on substantially identical rules — became the standard collateral instrument of American agriculture.

### 1933: The Commodity Credit Corporation Makes the Receipt the Heart of Farm Policy

The New Deal welded the warehouse receipt into the machinery of the state. The Commodity Credit Corporation, created in 1933, offered farmers nonrecourse marketing loans: deliver your crop to an approved warehouse, pledge the warehouse receipt, and receive the loan rate per pound or bushel. If prices rose, the farmer redeemed the receipt and sold; if they fell, the farmer forfeited the receipt and the government kept the crop. Every bale and bushel in the loan program required a receipt the government would accept at face value — which meant federal standards for warehouses, weighers, graders, and the documents themselves. For cotton in particular, the CCC loan made the receipt not just a trade document but the operational backbone of the industry, and any future change to the receipt would have to run through the CCC's requirements. That fact would shape the electronic transition profoundly.

### 1952: UCC Article 7 — The Receipt's Permanent Legal Home

The Uniform Commercial Code, promulgated in 1952 and adopted by the states over the following two decades, absorbed and replaced the UWRA. Article 7 codified the law of documents of title: the warehouseman's obligations as bailee, the warranties carried by the receipt, the magic of due negotiation (a holder by due negotiation takes title to the document and the goods, free of most adverse claims), and — critically for finance — perfection of a security interest in the goods by taking possession of a negotiable receipt. A merchant could pledge a vault full of bearer receipts to its bank simply by delivering them; no filing, no notation, no notice to the warehouse. The simplicity of possession-based perfection was the paper system's greatest feature, and replicating it electronically would become the central legal puzzle of the 1990s.

### 1963: The Salad Oil Swindle — The Eternal Lesson

In November 1963, the commodity world learned what happens when the verification behind the receipt fails. Anthony "Tino" De Angelis's Allied Crude Vegetable Oil Refining Corporation had borrowed enormously against field-warehouse receipts — many issued by a subsidiary of American Express — covering soybean oil in Bayonne, New Jersey tank farms. Much of the oil did not exist: tanks were topped with oil floating on seawater, interconnected so the same oil could be shown to successive inspectors, and receipts were forged outright. When the scheme collapsed, losses ran to roughly $150 million or more (well over a billion in today's dollars), dozens of firms were damaged, two brokerage houses failed, and American Express was nearly brought down. The scandal effectively killed the field-warehousing industry and burned a lesson into commodity finance that no later technology would erase: *a receipt — paper, electronic, or tokenized — is only as good as the physical verification, insurance, and custodian integrity behind it.*

### The Mature Paper System in Cotton — and Its Physical Limits

By the 1980s, the cotton receipt system was a marvel of mid-century industrial paperwork. Every one of America's ten-million-plus annual bales got its own negotiable receipt — typically a machine-readable punch card — issued by the warehouse, made out to bearer, and physically circulated: from warehouse to gin, gin to merchant, merchant to bank vault as loan collateral, bank to buyer against payment, and finally back to the warehouse for surrender when the bale shipped. The 1994 Memphis forum transcript preserves, in the participants' own words, exactly what was breaking:

**Physical fragility and logistics.** Receipts were flammable cards that could be bent, soaked, or mutilated; they consumed vault space; and every trade required physically moving them, with overnight-courier costs that were trivial next to the interest cost of cotton sitting unpaid while its title documents were in transit.

**Loss, theft, and forgery.** A lost receipt meant posting a bond or a letter of credit before the warehouse would ship. Plains Cotton Cooperative's Joe Tubb showed the room a real example: an Oklahoma bank's irrevocable letter of credit for $485,512.43, posted in October 1990 by a merchant who had lost 1,364 receipts — still outstanding four years later in case the "lost" bearer paper ever surfaced in someone else's hands. And because the receipt was a true bearer instrument, a wrongful holder could present it and ship the cotton; the document, not the person, carried the title.

**Dying machinery.** Dunavant's Louis Baioni told the forum that the card-handling machines the industry depended on were wearing out, that nobody manufactured them anymore, and that the engineers who knew how to repair them were literally dying off. The paper system was not merely inefficient; it was becoming physically unsupportable.

### February 1989: Six Football Fields of Paper

The event that converted the industry was an operational catastrophe born of success. On February 21, 1989, with a large Chinese purchase moving through the market, Plains Cotton Cooperative Association's Telecot electronic trading system sold 385,599 bales — roughly $110 million of cotton — in a single seven-hour session. The trades themselves settled smoothly, because equity trades moved only paper entitlements. The next week, after the CCC's adjusted world price moved, PCCA received loan redemption requests for 721,277 bales that had to be pulled, as physical punch-card receipts, from a servicing-agent bank inventory of about three million receipts.

Tubb did the arithmetic for the audience: three million receipts stacked end to end stretch the length of six football fields, and PCCA needed to extract a specific 150 yards' worth. The CCC, naturally, wanted its money immediately — PCCA wired roughly $145 million on Friday morning — but the merchants buying the cotton would not pay until they received the title documents. So PCCA's staff worked around the clock in shifts through Friday, Saturday, and Sunday, machine-sorting and hand-picking cards (one drawer of 70,000 receipts could not be machine-run at all). By Monday they had presented about 700,000 receipts; the final 21,000 took another two and a half weeks. The interest cost of carrying $145 million while the paper was found ran to roughly $50,000 per day. Tubb's summary to the forum was simple: that weekend was the moment PCCA decided to build electronic title. By the fall of 1989 it had a pilot running.

## Part II — Inventing the Electronic Warehouse Receipt (1984–1995)

The electronic warehouse receipt was not invented by a technology company and imposed on agriculture. It was demanded by the industry, built through a decade of pilots, and legalized one statute and one regulation at a time. The 1994 forum transcript is the best surviving record of that process, told by the people who did it.

### 1984–1986: A Bale-Tracking Request Becomes a Movement

In 1984, two segments of the cotton industry — producers and merchants, notably the parties at opposite ends of every transaction — approached the National Cotton Council with the same request: build us a bale tracking system. The Council spent 1985 on feasibility, commissioning a formal design study from EDS, which concluded that an electronic receipt system was achievable with mid-1980s technology. That same year the Council ran the first live electronic warehouse receipt test in Tunica, Mississippi, involving Federal Compress as the warehouse, a working producer, and First Tennessee Bank as the lender — proving from the very first experiment that the system had to satisfy a bank, not just a database. In 1986 the Council launched the Cotton Telecommunication Network, essentially an industry e-mail system, to get cotton's famously relationship-driven participants comfortable with electronic messages at all.

### 1989–1990: Pilots That Worked — Financially, Legally, and Technically

After the February 1989 paper crisis, the effort accelerated on two parallel tracks. The National Cotton Council ran a formal pilot in the fall of 1989 with Plains Cotton Cooperative and Panhandle Compress in West Texas — deliberately pairing a cooperative and an independent warehouse — writing purpose-built software, testing system security, and bringing in West Texas banks to actually lend against electronic title. The Council's Joe Wyrick was emphatic at the forum that these were never computer projects: every test had three legs — financial, legal, and technical — and all three had to hold. PCCA simultaneously took its own electronic titling system live with North Plant Cotton Compress, moving roughly 163,000 bales through the CCC loan electronically in the first season. In 1990 the pilot expanded to E. Ritter Warehouse in Marked Tree, Arkansas — the first test under a federally licensed warehouse, which mattered because federal licensure was where the legal change would have to begin.

The pilots also solved, in miniature, problems that would dog the system for years. When a buyer worried about paying before title moved, the 1989–90 pilots created an electronic bank draft: the seller transferred the receipts to the bank as a contingent holder; if the bank accepted, money and title moved simultaneously; if it rejected, the receipts reverted to the seller automatically. Settlement atomicity — delivery versus payment — was being engineered into warehouse receipts before the phrase "smart contract" existed.

### 1990–1992: Congress Amends the 1916 Act — Twice

The 1990 Farm Bill amended the United States Warehouse Act to permit federally licensed warehouses to issue electronic receipts under regulations USDA would write. The drafters believed they had covered the industry; they had not, because roughly half of commercial cotton warehouse capacity was state-licensed rather than federal. So the industry went back to Congress, and a 1992 amendment established that any warehouse holding a state or federal license could issue an electronic receipt that was the full legal equivalent of paper. Two features of the 1992 act proved decisive. First, it was strictly optional — no warehouse was forced to go electronic, and any depositor could demand a paper receipt at any time, with receipts convertible back and forth between paper and electronic form repeatedly over their life. Second, and most radically, it provided that whoever a USDA-authorized provider's system designated as *holder* of an electronic receipt would be *considered to be in possession* of a warehouse receipt for purposes of perfecting interests under federal and state law. In one sentence, Congress translated the entire possession-based edifice of UCC Article 7 into a database field — a move John McQuiston, EWR Inc.'s lawyer, explained to skeptical merchants at the forum as superseding state law precisely so that banks could finance against a computer entry.

### March 31 – May 2, 1994: The Federal Rules Arrive

USDA's Agricultural Stabilization and Conservation Service (ASCS — folded into the new Farm Service Agency just weeks after the Memphis forum) published the EWR regulations in the Federal Register on March 31, 1994; they took effect May 2. ASCS's Steve Mikkelsen told the forum that one phrase had governed the entire design effort: "it's just like paper." The regulations deliberately replicated the paper world's mechanics rather than reimagining them, to minimize disruption:

The *bearer* of a paper receipt became the *holder* of an electronic one — a single designated entity at a time, with all the rights and privileges of a paper holder, and the only party able to transfer the receipt onward. Electronic receipts had to be issued one per bale, mirroring paper practice. A receipt was legally *issued* the moment the warehouse's data entered the provider's central filing system. The data on a receipt split into two legal categories: the statutory fields required by Section 18 of the USWA (which only the issuing warehouse could correct, by having the receipt transferred back to it), and *additional information* — everything else, beginning with the holder field itself, which holders could freely annotate for purposes like CCC loans or exchange certification. The receipt never moved; only the holder designation changed.

The provider — the new species of entity that would run the central computer — faced a regime of obligations: an agreement with USDA renewing annually each April 30; a minimum net worth of $25,000; two insurance policies of at least $2 million each, one for errors and omissions and one for fraud and dishonesty; a $2,000 application fee and $2,000 annual fee (an early proposal of a one-dollar government fee per receipt had been beaten back); an annual audited financial statement and an annual electronic data processing audit covering operations, security, and disaster recovery; published, nondiscriminatory fee schedules; on-site physical security; and daily disaster-recovery records kept in fireproof storage with one set off-site. USDA would not limit the number of providers or set their prices, would not regulate access except to ban discrimination among users, and took no responsibility for state-licensed warehouses — state law continued to govern them, with the federal system simply accepting their filings.

On July 15, 1994, the Kansas City commodity office mailed out 24 provider application packets to applicants ranging from major software companies to individual warehousemen. By the day of the forum, August 29, not one had come back. Everyone in the room understood the 1994 crop would still move on paper; 1995 would be the first electronic year.

### August 29, 1994: The Memphis Forum — An Industry Argues Itself Into the Future

The American Cotton Shippers Association's Electronic Warehouse Receipt Forum, transcribed by a court reporter at the industry's request, assembled every constituency — the Council, the regulator, three would-be providers, merchants, warehousemen, four banks, the insurance trade, and the New York Cotton Exchange — to stress-test the new system in public. It deserves close reading because nearly every issue raised that day is still the live agenda for digital assets thirty years later.

**The utility model.** Tom Scott explained why the National Bank of Commerce, after years of eyeing EWR provision as a profit opportunity, concluded that the economics didn't work: the industry would not pay the 50-to-75-cents-per-receipt price a for-profit provider would need against the risks involved, so the answer was a utility. EWR, Inc. — chartered just the week before — was organized by a cross-section of the industry's largest names: Dunavant, Allenberg, Staplcotn, Hohenberg, Continental's cotton arm, Memphis Compress, Federal Compress, Tensas, Hollybrook, Royster, Trinity in West Texas, Union Services. It would raise one to two million dollars, return 80 to 90 percent of investors' capital over three to five years with a small return, plow any excess back into lower per-bale fees, and govern itself through industry committees on security, technology, and audit. It already had roughly six million bales pre-subscribed. The explicit design goal was that nobody profit from the title layer — the same instinct that, decades later, would drive consortium and public-utility designs for tokenized market infrastructure.

**The engineering.** Joe Tubb's account of PCCA's five-year-old production system set the security bar: a hardened, combination-locked computer site with Halon fire suppression; a Department of Defense-rated access-control package logging every change to the second with operator, program, and terminal identity; dedicated leased lines (no dial-up), with encrypted FM radio links as backup; nightly backups couriered to an underground bank vault; a negotiated hot site in Seattle promising recovery within 24 hours; and a full-time security administrator enforcing two four-inch standards manuals. The system's reliability record reframed the risk question: in the prior crop year PCCA had suffered 26 minutes of software downtime — and 582 minutes of telephone-line outages. Since 1989 it had handled 8.5 million bales electronically with zero bonds and zero letters of credit issued for lost title, and USDA audits had repeatedly concluded electronic title was as good as or better collateral than physical receipts. David Cox of Intelligent Decisions, Inc. — the industry's largest software house, processing over half the U.S. crop at some stage, and pointedly owning no interest in any cotton — described an architecture of four physically and logically separated systems (the receipt database, inbound communications, outbound communications, billing), real-time mirrored writes across duplicate hardware with automatic failover, and parallel electronic and paper audit trails. His cost estimate: creating an EWR would run 10 to 15 cents, *cheaper than printing the paper card it replaced*.

**The skeptics.** The transcript's intellectual center is Robert Weil, Sr., of Weil Brothers — a firm that had used the bearer receipt for 116 years — who read into the record a catechism of roughly two dozen questions that remains the canonical due-diligence list for any digitized title system. Can an electronic record truly be a bearer instrument, with the same negotiability and legal support? Can it carry the warehouse's warranties — shelter, insurance, merchantable delivery — that made the paper receipt a contract as well as a title? Can it be pledged in escrow, recorded with liens, kept confidential from everyone but the holder? Who guarantees against error, lost records, computer fraud, viruses, or an insider at the provider colluding with a failing merchant to reassign bales (a scenario he sketched in chilling operational detail)? What are the provider's resources when something goes wrong — and here he pronounced the regulatory minimums "woefully inadequate," comparing them to the Depository Trust Company, which stood behind securities settlement with a participants' fund near $200 million, hundreds of millions more callable from members, daily reconciliation across 1,200 participants, and the collective net worth of Wall Street behind it. His son, Robert Weil II, pressed the same point: clearinghouses survive failures because their users mutualize losses without limit; a thinly capitalized private provider does not. The Weils were not opposing electronic receipts — the family firm would buy electronic cotton happily — they were demanding that the trust machinery be rebuilt at full strength before the paper machinery was dismantled.

**The bankers.** Union Planters' David Colby spoke for cautious lenders everywhere: banks perfect by *dominion and control* — physical possession of the collateral documents — and could not yet take the electronic product to counsel for an opinion, did not know how money and title would move simultaneously in the transition, and would still need printed records for auditors. NBC's Bill Butler was more bullish, with a devastating aside about the paper system everyone professed to trust: a negotiable receipt is a promise from a stranger hundreds of miles away, backed in one real example by a $250,000 bond on a warehouse holding $34 million of cotton. ABN AMRO's Dick West, from the New York syndication world, identified the providers as clearinghouses that would ultimately control both the receipts and significant cash flows, argued their creditworthiness was the whole game, and — like Butler — favored a single DTC-like provider over a fragmented field. Rabobank's representative noted that metals already traded on electronic confirmations and that banks would adapt; the problem was education, not principle.

**The structural critiques.** Merchant James Loeb attacked the regulations' competitive design: because a holder could not unilaterally move a receipt from one provider to another, the "new industry called providers" would face no real price competition — a captive market like warehouse storage tariffs. He demanded, and the panel conceded, that paper receipts remain available at no additional cost. Country merchant Dan Osborne raised what might be called the last-mile problem through a constituent he named from the floor: a ten-bale farmer with no computer — and no television. The answers (free conversion to paper on demand, agents acting for holders, the suggestion that government county offices might key loan entries for small producers) established that any title system must keep an analog door open for participants who cannot or will not digitize. Susan Hackney of Mask Cotton Company drilled into settlement: at what instant does money move against title, and what happens when the warehouse can ship only 89 of a 90-bale order — questions answered partly by the pilot's electronic draft mechanism and partly by an admission that intermediated trust would still do the work. The insurance industry, through Forrest Lipscomb, announced it would simply wait: marine cotton policies insured the bale, not the receipt, and underwriters would demand paper proof of ownership for the first year or so until the system proved itself. The New York Cotton Exchange's Donald Conlin protected the futures delivery mechanism: with receipts passing through eight or nine hands inside a four-day delivery window, hard copies would remain mandatory for certificated stock until the electronic system was universal. And David Cox surfaced a uniquely 1990s wrinkle — PCCA held a patent it read to cover combining electronic warehouse receipts with electronic marketing, which the Council would sublicense to providers, an early example of intellectual property friction in market infrastructure.

What is striking in retrospect is how completely the room's collective judgment held up. The consensus closing the meeting — voiced by ACSA's president, Winford McGehee — was that the industry did not yet have confidence in the regulations as written and should comment them into shape; that enormous education lay ahead; and that the merchants, bankers, and providers each had homework. All of that happened. And the specific demands made that day — over-built security, mutualized utility economics, a paper escape hatch, conversion rights, agent access for small holders, audit trails to the second — became the actual architecture of the system that conquered the cotton trade.

### 1995 and After: Live

The first commercial EWRs were issued for the 1995 crop. The Commodity Credit Corporation signed on as a user of the provider systems, connecting them to its Kansas City computers so a producer's loan request could flow from gin to warehouse to provider to CCC and back to the county office in a continuous electronic loop. With the loan program, the merchants, the warehouses, and the dominant providers all aligned — and with EWR creation genuinely cheaper than printing punch cards — adoption in cotton was rapid and essentially irreversible. Within a few crop years, electronic title was the norm; within a decade, paper cotton receipts were a curiosity requested mainly for conversions, claims, and the occasional traditionalist.

## Part III — Maturation and Legal Consolidation (1995–2023)

### 1999: Metals Go Electronic in Parallel

Cotton was not alone. In 1999 the London Metal Exchange launched LMEsword, a central electronic depository for metal warrants — the warehouse receipts deliverable against LME contracts — replacing the physical circulation of bearer warrants among banks and brokers in the City. The design choice was the same one American cotton had made: a trusted central registry recording a single holder per warrant, not a distributed or cryptographic system. By the 2000s, the central-registry electronic receipt had become the global template for exchange-grade commodities.

### October 1999: From Single-Bale to Multiple-Bale Receipts

The 1994 regulations had imposed a rule that every cotton EWR be a single-bale receipt — a faithful translation of decades of one-card-per-bale paper practice. But cotton is not handled identically across the country, and warehouse operators in some regions wanted to receipt lotted cotton more efficiently. After a 1996 proposed rule and public comment, the Farm Service Agency issued a final rule effective October 7, 1999 (amending 7 CFR Part 735) that removed the single-bale-only requirement and authorized warehouses to issue single- *and* multiple-bale receipts in either paper or electronic form. It also discarded an older constraint that multiple-bale paper receipts represent between 25 and 200 bales, letting storage and tagging "be conducted as efficiency dictates." The continuity of the cast is worth noting: the rule's contact official was Steve Mikkelsen — the same ASCS official who had walked the Memphis forum through the brand-new regulations five years earlier — by then Deputy Director of the Warehouse and Inventory Division.

The rule is most revealing in what it refused to relax. Several commenters — warehouses, a provider, a merchant — argued that the individual bale tags and weights for a lotted receipt should be kept on file at the warehouse rather than carried on the multiple-bale receipt itself. USDA rejected that, holding that Section 18(f) of the USWA (7 U.S.C. 260) requires every receipt to describe its bales "by marks, numbers, or other means of identification and the weight," so bale-level identity must remain part of the receipt record even when bales are aggregated. That insistence — that the receipt always resolve down to the individual bale's fingerprint and weight — is the statutory backbone of the per-bale data discipline (PBI tags, verified weights) that makes cotton title so reliable, and it is the reason the modern multiple-bale EWR still carries a tag list rather than a bare bale count.

### 2000: The Warehouse Act Rewritten for the Digital Age

The Grain Standards and Warehouse Improvement Act of 2000 (Public Law 106-472) replaced the text of the 1916 USWA wholesale. Where the 1990 and 1992 amendments had grafted electronic receipts onto a paper statute, the 2000 Act was written *around* them: it gave explicit statutory authority for electronic warehouse receipts and other electronic USWA documents across *all* agricultural commodities, formalized the provider agreement and the central filing system (CFS) as the legal architecture, and modernized USDA's licensing and enforcement tools. The current regulations — today at 7 CFR Part 869 — define the CFS as an electronic system operated by a provider acting as a disinterested, neutral third party, independent of outside influence in action or appearance, with USDA setting annual user fees and retaining unlimited audit access. Cotton's experiment had become the law of the land for grain, rice, peanuts, and beyond.

### 2003: UCC Article 7 Catches Up

State commercial law followed federal warehouse law. In 2003 the American Law Institute and Uniform Law Commission revised UCC Article 7 to put electronic documents of title on equal footing with paper. The revision's central invention was *control* (Section 7-106) as the electronic analogue of possession: a system reliably establishing a single authoritative copy of the record, identifying the person in control, and making the record alterable only with that person's consent gives its controller everything possession of paper once gave — due negotiation, holder-in-due-course-style protection, and perfection of security interests by control rather than filing. Revised Article 7 also blessed conversion between paper and electronic form in both directions, exactly as the cotton system had practiced since 1994. States adopted the revision over the following decade, giving the EWR a general commercial-law home alongside its federal one.

### The 2000s–2010s: Beyond Cotton, and Total Conversion Within It

After the 2002 farm bill dismantled the peanut quota system, peanuts moved onto electronic receipts using the same provider model, and grain and rice EWRs were authorized under the 2000 Act. Adoption outside cotton, however, has always been partial: grain's fragmented elevator landscape, multiple state indemnity regimes, and lender conservatism kept a large share of grain title on paper or on non-negotiable scale tickets. USDA still maintains a form for ordering *printed* warehouse receipt books — bearer, order, or non-negotiable — because paper never fully died.

Cotton, by contrast, completed the conversion. By the mid-2010s the EWR was, in Pozez's description, the sole practical evidence of stored cotton's existence, quality, and quantity, and EWR, Inc. — the Memphis-born utility of 1994, by then headquartered in Collierville, Tennessee — was issuing 100 percent of the EWRs for cotton warehoused in the United States, while also serving as a USDA-approved provider for peanuts and grain.

### The Mature System, circa 2016: How a Bale Becomes a Receipt

Pozez's 2016 study documents the system at full maturity, and it is worth tracing the lifecycle because it shows how deeply data integrity had been engineered into the physical supply chain.

A farmer packs harvested cotton into modules of up to 25,000 pounds, each tagged with a unique identifier and accompanied by an electronic data file (seed variety, producer, field) to the gin. The gin — legally acting as the farmer's agent — separates fiber from seed, presses standard bales (the Gin Universal Density standard: 500 pounds, 55 by 21 by 33 inches), and affixes to each bale and each sample a *permanent bale indicator*: a barcoded PBI tag and number that Pozez calls the bale's fingerprint. The gin transmits bale-level data files (gin and farm identifiers, weights, compression status) to the receiving warehouse and to a USDA classing office, mostly over the Stamp Airmail data network operated by the same company behind the EWR platform. The classing office instrument-tests every bale's sample — fiber length, leaf, micronaire, strength, per federal regulation — and returns grade and class electronically.

The warehouse scans each arriving bale's PBI tag, physically verifies it against the gin's data, stores it, and then creates the EWR by entering the gin data, the classing data, and its own particulars (receipt number, storage date, verified weights, storage location) into the provider's platform. The provider runs automated validation before issuance: required fields must be present, and values must sit within tolerances — a bale flagged as Gin Universal Density that is recorded at 400 pounds is rejected, because that compression standard means 500 pounds. Only when the file validates does the system issue the receipt and notify the holder and warehouse. The first holder is typically the gin, as the farmer's agent; it may also be the farmer, a merchant, a draft bank, or a collateral bank.

Transfer is a few fields and a few seconds: the holder enters the warehouse ID, the EWR number, and the new holder, and confirmations go to all parties. Shipping reverses the process: the holder lodges a shipping order through the system; the warehouse inspects the bales against their receipts, loads the trucks, and must cancel the EWRs within 24 hours of the bales' departure — with any cancellation reversal requiring verification by the warehouse's licensing authority, a deliberate anti-fraud checkpoint. From the loading dock onward, a multimodal bill of lading becomes the operative document of title; the EWR's life ends at the warehouse door.

Two financial structures deserve note. The platform itself never touches money: settlement is the parties' business, with *draft banks* serving as escrow intermediaries — taking holdership of the EWR from the seller, confirming terms with both sides through the system, collecting from the buyer, paying the seller, and passing the receipt — a manual, trusted re-creation of delivery-versus-payment. And for lending, the system supports a two-tier *holder / sub-holder* structure: the collateral bank is the holder of record, the borrowing owner is sub-holder; the sub-holder can market the cotton, but every transfer or shipping order routes to the bank for electronic approval or rejection, and the warehouse will not release a collateralized bale until the provider relays the bank's consent. Possession-as-perfection, translated into workflow.

Oversight, finally, is layered and redundant. Roughly 53 percent of commercial cotton warehouse capacity in the period ran under state licenses and 47 percent under the USWA, but the rulebooks converged to near-identical language: no two receipts may bear the same number; warehouses may issue only consecutively allotted receipt numbers from their licensing authority; and both the provider and the warehouses must grant regulators unlimited, free access to all records at any time. The provider runs its own integrity reports continuously, and warehousemen run parallel ones — a culture, Pozez observed, in which "parties do business on a smile and handshake" precisely because the verification machinery underneath is relentless.

### 2017–2023: Administrative Modernization

In the late 2010s, administration of the USWA moved from the Farm Service Agency to the Agricultural Marketing Service, with the regulations recodified at 7 CFR Part 869, and AMS refreshed the provider paperwork. The current cotton addendum to the provider agreement — Form WA-460-1, revised October 2023 — is a quietly revealing snapshot of three decades of accumulated pragmatism. The 1994 single-bale-only rule has given way to recognized *non-negotiable multiple-bale* EWRs alongside single-bale negotiables. License-type codes on every receipt now include not just federal, state, and unlicensed warehouses but warehouses licensed by ICE Futures U.S., Inc. — with country ISO codes for foreign locations — because the cotton futures contract, and therefore the receipt system, has internationalized. The pure 1994 doctrine that only the holder can change a receipt has acquired two narrow, sensible exceptions: AMS may update the storage-paid-through date and receiving-charge status on receipts held by the CCC once the government has actually paid the warehouse, and the warehouse may update a bale's physical location without holder consent (with notice to the holder) — because bales get moved within sheds and the record should follow reality. Conversion rules require the EWR number to be printed on the face of any paper receipt issued from an electronic one, preserving the audit chain across media. And the provider must interface with USDA's warehouse examiners' software, so the regulator's field staff can audit the CFS directly.

The companion document on the warehouse side — the Licensing Agreement for Cotton Warehouse Operators, Form WA-401 (rev. October 2023) — sets out the obligations the receipt rests on, and reading it alongside the 1994 forum shows how durable the original design choices proved. The warehouse operator's bailee duties are entirely independent of whether its receipts are paper or electronic: full-replacement insurance against fire, lightning, windstorm and the like attaching the moment cotton enters custody; a system of accounts cross-referencing every bale tag to its receipt; daily backup of electronic records to fireproof storage; six-year record retention; consecutive receipt numbers drawn from AMS; and a flat prohibition on releasing any cotton until the warehouse is the final holder of the EWR (or holds the surrendered paper). This is precisely the point Neal Gillen pressed at the forum — that a provider is "just a custodial service" and the warehouseman's underlying bailor responsibility is untouched — now codified line by line. The WA-401 also quantifies duties the forum could only argue about: the obligation to deliver "without unnecessary delay" is defined as shipping or staging at least 4.5 percent of capacity per week, and a liquidation-of-claims waterfall (receipted and verifiable storage obligations first, then disputed obligations, then lien-holder claims for warehouse charges, then warehouse-owned cotton) gives the concrete recourse ordering that Robert Weil demanded in 1994.

## Part IV — How Warehouse Receipts Are Used Now (2026)

### The United States

**Cotton: a fully electronic title system.** Essentially every bale of U.S. cotton in commercial storage today lives as an EWR in a USDA-authorized central filing system. USDA-AMS's approved-provider list for cotton currently includes EWR, Inc. (also approved for peanuts and grain) and — fittingly — Plains Cotton Cooperative Association, the pioneer of 1989, though EWR, Inc. has historically issued effectively all cotton receipts. The receipt is the operating system of the trade: gins issue to themselves as producers' agents; merchants accumulate holderships as they buy; collateral banks sit as holders over sub-holder borrowers; the CCC takes holdership for marketing assistance loans; ICE Futures U.S. certificated stocks ride on the same rails; and shipping orders, cancellations, and invoices all flow through the provider platform within regulated time limits.

**Other commodities: a mixed economy.** AMS maintains provider-agreement addenda not just for cotton but for grain, rice, and even coffee, and the approved-provider roster spans EWR, Inc. (grain and peanuts), eGrain, Inc. (grain and rice), and eCOPS — the electronic commodity operations system associated with ICE — covering coffee, cocoa, orange juice, and even ocean bills of lading for exchange-graded softs. But adoption outside cotton remains uneven: substantial grain volume still moves on paper receipts and non-negotiable instruments, and USDA still prints serially numbered paper receipt books to order. The pattern is consistent worldwide: electronic receipts dominate where a concentrated industry, a dominant financing program, or an exchange forces standardization, and paper persists in the fragmented remainder.

**The functional roles today.** Four jobs define the modern EWR. It is *loan collateral* — for the CCC's marketing assistance loans and for commercial lenders perfecting by holdership/control rather than filing. It is the *settlement instrument* of physical trade — holdership transferring against payment, with draft banks or correspondent arrangements bridging the cash leg. It is the *audit and inventory backbone* — regulators, lenders, and merchants reconcile their books against the CFS daily, the modern version of the stock checks PCCA invented in 1989. And it is the *exchange delivery vehicle* for certificated stocks.

### Internationally

The American provider-registry model has been widely emulated. India built a statutory electronic negotiable warehouse receipt (eNWR) regime under its Warehousing (Development and Regulation) Act, with two licensed repositories operating since 2017 and pledge finance against eNWRs growing rapidly. Metals exchanges in London, New York, and Asia run central electronic warrant systems. Development institutions — the World Bank, FAO, IFC — actively promote warehouse receipt systems and their digitization across Africa, Asia, and Latin America as a route to crop finance for smallholders, in markets where receipts are often still paper, sometimes unregulated, and occasionally the instrument of fraud rather than its cure. The 2014 Qingdao scandal in China — in which the same metal stocks were pledged to multiple banks through duplicate paper receipts, leaving international lenders with roughly $3 billion of exposure — became the global advertisement both for centralized electronic registries and, later, for blockchain-based alternatives.

## Part V — The Limits of Today's Electronic Receipt

The EWR system is one of the great unsung successes of market digitization: fraud-resistant, cheap, fast, and so reliable that it became invisible. Its limitations are real, but they are the limitations of a 1994 design philosophy — "just like paper," implemented as a trusted central registry — meeting 2026 expectations.

**Centralization and concentration.** The system's trust anchor is institutional, not technological: a single neutral provider per commodity, kept honest by USDA agreements, insurance minimums, annual audits, and reputation. That model has worked for thirty years, but it concentrates operational, cyber, and continuity risk in one database and one company — the very single-point-of-failure concern Robert Weil raised in 1994 with his Depository Trust Company comparison, and the competition concern James Loeb raised when he observed that holders could not move receipts between providers. Loeb's complaint was eventually answered only in part: under the current rules (Form WA-401), a *warehouse operator* may, with 30 days' written AMS approval and 30 days' notice to all holders, have its current provider transfer its entire EWR dataset from one central filing system to another — but only once per year, and only at the warehouse's initiative, not the individual holder's. So provider portability exists at the warehouse level and on an annual cadence; the holder-level, market-driven price competition Loeb wanted never materialized, and concentration was instead managed by regulation and good behavior. A provider outage, compromise, or insolvency would today freeze title to an entire crop, with recovery dependent on disaster-recovery records and the provider's modest mandated capital and insurance — figures whose 1994 levels Weil called woefully inadequate against the value at risk, and whose warehouse-side counterpart (financial assurance capped at $250,000 per state, computed at roughly $10 per bale) remains, just as Bill Butler observed in 1994, a small fraction of a full warehouse's cotton value.

**Closed registries and weak interoperability.** Each central filing system is a silo with its own formats, interfaces, and user agreements. Receipts do not move freely between providers — a holder cannot redirect a receipt to a competing CFS, and even the warehouse-level transfer the rules allow is a once-a-year, AMS-approved migration of a warehouse's whole book, not a fluid market; data integration with banks, merchants, and the CCC happens through batch files, portals, and bespoke connections; and confirmations have historically traveled by fax and e-mail. The system digitized the *record* but only partially digitized the *workflows* around it.

**The receipt stops at the warehouse door.** An EWR's legal life ends when the bales ship and the receipt is canceled; a multimodal bill of lading takes over, and the transaction re-enters the older, slower world of ocean documents, courier packets, and letters of credit. The EWR has no native legal existence abroad: it is a creature of the USWA and state law, recognized domestically by statute, but a foreign bank financing the same cotton afloat is back to paper or to private-platform workarounds. For a commodity in which most U.S. production is exported, this is the sharpest functional boundary.

**No atomic settlement.** By deliberate design, the provider platform carries no money. Delivery versus payment is simulated through draft banks, correspondent escrow, and trust — the structures Susan Hackney probed in 1994 and Pozez found still doing the work in 2016, in an industry that famously documents nine-figure trades with handshakes. The gap between title transfer and cash transfer is small, intermediated, and almost always benign; it is also exactly the seam that modern market-infrastructure design exists to close.

**The oracle problem.** An electronic registry proves, beyond practical dispute, *who holds the claim*. It cannot prove *that the cotton exists*, that it matches its grade, or that the warehouse is solvent and honest. Those assurances still come from the physical layer: licensed weighers and graders, PBI tags and classing samples, warehouse examinations, bonds, and insurance. The salad-oil tanks of 1963 and the Qingdao sheds of 2014 both defeated their eras' paperwork; an EWR database, however immaculate, is equally hostage to the integrity of what happens at the loading dock. Every digitization of the receipt inherits this limit, and tokenization will too.

**Whole-lot, business-hours instruments.** EWRs are single-bale (or defined-lot) claims administered in systems built for crop-season workflows. There is no fractional interest in a receipt, no programmatic collateral logic beyond the holder/sub-holder approval workflow, no 24/7 transferability, and no native way to plug stored-commodity collateral into modern electronic funding markets — repo, tokenized money funds, automated margining — without manual intermediation.

**Legal seams at the frontier.** The 2003 Article 7 control test was drafted around the single-authoritative-copy registry model and fit it perfectly; it fit distributed-ledger records awkwardly, since a blockchain deliberately has no single copy. Until the 2022 UCC amendments supplied a technology-flexible control concept, a tokenized receipt sat in a gray zone between Article 7's documents of title and the general-intangibles residue of Article 9 — a gap that mattered to exactly the constituency that has always decided this document's fate: the lenders.

## Part VI — The Tokenized Future

### What a Tokenized Warehouse Receipt Would Be

Tokenization, properly understood, is not scanning the receipt or pointing a token at a database row. It is making the token *be* the receipt: a controllable electronic record on a ledger where exclusive control of the token — typically via cryptographic keys — *is* holdership, where transfer is a ledger transaction that is final, timestamped, and globally visible to permitted parties, and where the ledger itself is the filing system. Conceptually it is the third great re-implementation of the same instrument: the bearer paper of 1916 located title in physical possession; the EWR of 1994 located it in a designated database field maintained by a trusted provider; the token locates it in cryptographic control, with the trust requirement shifted from one operator's database to a ledger's consensus rules and the legal wrapper around them. Two architectures compete: permissioned ledgers operated by or for regulated providers (a central filing system re-platformed on shared-ledger technology, where users might barely notice the change), and public-chain tokens issued under legal frameworks that bind the token to the goods. Both exist today in adjacent markets.

### The Legal Scaffolding Is Largely Built (2017–2026)

The decisive development of the past decade is that the law has moved first — unusually, ahead of large-scale deployment.

Internationally, UNCITRAL's Model Law on Electronic Transferable Records (2017) provides functional equivalence for the whole family of transferable documents — bills of lading, promissory notes, and warehouse receipts expressly among them — using *control through a reliable system* as the substitute for possession, in deliberate echo of the American EWR statutes. MLETR-based legislation has been enacted by a growing list of jurisdictions including Bahrain, Singapore, the UAE's financial centers, Belize, Paraguay, Papua New Guinea, Kiribati, and, most consequentially, the United Kingdom, whose Electronic Trade Documents Act took effect in September 2023 — significant far beyond Britain because English law governs so much of world trade documentation. France followed as the first EU member to fully transpose MLETR, with enabling legislation in 2024 completed by decree in 2025, and Germany, Japan, and others are moving through study and drafting stages. The legal possibility of a warehouse receipt that is a token, recognized across borders, now exists in a meaningful share of world trade law.

In the United States, the 2022 amendments to the Uniform Commercial Code did the domestic equivalent. A new Article 12 creates the *controllable electronic record* (CER): a digital asset susceptible of exclusive control, transferable with negotiability-like protection — a good-faith qualifying purchaser who obtains control takes free of competing claims, the take-free magic of due negotiation reborn for tokens — with perfection of security interests by control beating perfection by filing. Companion amendments modernized Article 7's control test for electronic documents of title so that distributed-ledger systems can qualify, not just single-copy registries. By early 2026, more than thirty states plus the District of Columbia had enacted the amendments; New York — the law governing most U.S. financial contracts — signed them in December 2025, effective June 3, 2026, days before this writing. The commercial-law rails for a tokenized warehouse receipt are, as of this month, substantially laid in the world's largest financial jurisdiction.

Two further American ingredients matter. The federal stablecoin framework enacted in 2025 and regulators' parallel work on accepting tokenized assets as collateral in derivatives and funding markets are building the *cash leg* — the tokenized dollar or deposit that a receipt token would settle against. And the 1992 USWA precedent looms large: Congress once declared that whatever a USDA-authorized provider's system says about holdership counts as possession nationwide. The same statutory machinery — provider agreements, central filing systems, AMS rulemaking — could authorize a ledger-based CFS without waiting on any further act of Congress. The warehouse receipt is, oddly, one of the few asset classes where the federal tokenization hook has existed since the Clinton administration.

### Working Precedents

The future is already running in adjacent lanes. The clearest live tokenized warehouse receipts in the world are the gold tokens — PAX Gold (2019) and Tether Gold (2020) — each token a redeemable claim on allocated bullion in audited London vaults, trading globally around the clock with billions of dollars outstanding: economically a bearer vault receipt reborn, with all the custody verification (and key-loss risk) that implies. In agriculture, Argentina's Agrotoken built the first grain-token infrastructure: SOYA, CORA, and WHEA tokens, each representing one tonne of grain actually delivered to a participating elevator and verified through a proof-of-grain-reserve attestation, listed alongside the Matba Rofex futures market, spendable with agribusiness merchants via a card, and — in a 2022 first with Santander — accepted as collateral for bank loans locked in smart contracts, with expansion toward Brazil. A farmer's stored crop becoming spendable, pledgeable digital collateral on a phone is the EWR's promise completed.

Around the receipt, the trade-digitization ecosystem has consolidated through a brutal Darwinian decade: the agribusiness majors' Covantis platform digitized bulk-grain post-trade execution on enterprise blockchain; komgo digitized commodity trade finance documentation for banks and traders; electronic bill-of-lading platforms certified under MLETR/ETDA standards are scaling — while celebrated consortium ventures (TradeLens, we.trade, Marco Polo, Contour) died of governance and adoption economics rather than technology. Their epitaphs read like minutes from Memphis 1994: infrastructure for an industry survives when it is owned as a utility by the industry it serves, priced near cost, aligned with the regulator, and launched with the biggest users pre-committed — the EWR, Inc. formula, rediscovered expensively.

### What Tokenization Could Add

Measured against Part V's limitations, the token's contributions map one to one. *Atomic settlement:* a receipt token and a stablecoin or tokenized deposit exchanged in a single conditional transaction collapses the draft-bank float and answers, with finality, Susan Hackney's 1994 question of when money moves against title. *Programmable collateral:* the holder/sub-holder approval workflow becomes a smart contract — the lien releases automatically on repayment, the bank's "push a button" of 1994 made literal, instant, and incapable of being forgotten; CCC loan mechanics, storage-charge waterfalls, and margin calls are all expressible as code on the receipt itself. *Fractionalization and composability:* tonne-level or bale-level claims can be divided, pooled, and posted into modern funding markets — repo against grain, money-market integration, exchange margin — the direction U.S. regulators' tokenized-collateral work already points. *Borderlessness:* under MLETR-aligned law, the token that is a Memphis warehouse receipt can be controlled, pledged, and transferred by a buyer in Singapore with the same legal effect as local possession, and can hand off cryptographically to an electronic bill of lading instead of dying at the warehouse door. *Richer provenance:* the PBI tag, classing data, condition sensors, and sustainability attestations can be hashed to the token, making the receipt carry not just title but verifiable history — the traceability premium modern textile and food buyers pay for.

### What Tokenization Does Not Solve — Weil's Questions Return

Honesty requires the other column, and the 1994 transcript supplies it almost verbatim. *The oracle problem stands untouched:* a token can be as empty as a salad-oil tank, and a proof-of-reserve attestation is only as good as the attester at the elevator; licensed weighers, examiners, insurance, and physical audit remain the foundation, and tokenization arguably raises the stakes by letting a fraudulent claim travel farther, faster, with stronger finality. *Bearer risk returns:* the industry deliberately chose the accountable "holder" over the anonymous "bearer" in 1994 partly because a bearer instrument in the wrong hands ships the cotton — and a private key is the most bearer-like instrument ever devised; key custody, multi-signature controls, and recovery mechanisms are the new vault, and a stolen key is a stolen crop with cryptographic finality. *Governance and recourse:* who freezes a token after a court order, reverses an insider fraud, or makes victims whole — and with what capital — is precisely Weil's demand for DTC-grade, participant-funded backstops, now relitigated as debates over token recovery, validator governance, and issuer insurance; his insider-collusion scenario translates without edits into a compromised oracle or admin key. *Concentration migrates rather than disappears:* from one provider's database to one chain's validators, one custodian's keys, one bridge's code. And *Jethro Nunn is now a farmer without a wallet:* the ten-bale producer with no computer and no television has a 2026 counterpart, and the answers will rhyme with 1994's — agents and custodians acting for holders, conversion rights back to simpler forms, institutional on-ramps through co-ops and county offices, and an analog door kept open. The questions read into the record in Memphis on August 29, 1994 are, with the nouns updated, the institutional due-diligence checklist for tokenized real-world assets today — which is the strongest evidence that the industry already knows how to answer them.

### A Plausible Path

The likely road is evolutionary and, by crypto standards, boring — which is the highest compliment in title infrastructure. Near term: USDA-authorized providers re-platform or shadow their central filing systems on ledger rails behind existing interfaces, so that holdership becomes control without users changing workflows; pilots tokenize the most standardized pools first — exchange-certificated stocks and CCC loan collateral — where a single counterparty (the exchange, the government) can mandate uniformity; and the maturing tokenized-cash stack supplies the settlement leg. Medium term: MLETR-recognized receipt tokens interoperate with electronic bills of lading so title flows unbroken from gin to mill across borders; emerging-market warehouse receipt systems, with less legacy to defend, leapfrog directly to tokenized registries under development-finance sponsorship, as Latin American grain tokens already suggest; and fractional, programmable commodity collateral plugs into regulated funding markets. Throughout, expect the surviving architecture to look institutionally familiar: permissioned or legally wrapped, regulator-supervised, mutually owned or utility-priced, over-insured, audited continuously, and equipped with a paper conversion right nobody uses but everybody demands — because that is the shape of every title system that has actually worked.

## Conclusion: One Document, Five Technologies, the Same Bargain

Across four millennia the warehouse receipt has been a clay tablet, a goldsmith's note, an engraved bearer card, a punch card, a database row, and — imminently — a token. The instrument never changed; only the technology of trust did. Each generation re-solved the identical problem: making a claim on absent goods as dependable as the goods themselves. Each transition was triggered by an operational breaking point — the 1914 credit collapse that produced the federal license, the 1989 weekend when $145 million waited on six football fields of punch cards, today's siloed, non-atomic, border-bound registries straining against globalized, programmable finance. And each new system was forced, by its skeptics, to rebuild the full trust stack before the old one was retired: verification of the physical goods, accountability of the custodian, capital behind the operator, recourse when things go wrong, and an exit to the previous medium.

The American cotton industry's archive — a court reporter's transcript from Memphis, a law-review roadmap of the mature system, a USDA form quietly accreting three decades of fixes — shows exactly how such a transition succeeds: pilot for a decade before mandating anything; write the law before the crop comes in; let the regulator and the industry draft together; run the title layer as a utility no one profits from; over-build security past the minimums; keep the paper door open for the smallest participant; and treat every skeptic's question as a design requirement. Tokenization will not need to discover these principles. It will need only the humility to reread them — because the bale, the bank, and the question of whether the cotton is really there have not changed since 1916, and they will not change on a blockchain either.
